Lesson 2: Proactive Strategies

Preventing Disruptions Through Design

Creating a peaceful classroom doesn’t start with discipline; it starts with design. The most effective teachers don't wait for problems to happen; they plan ahead to prevent them. This lesson is all about how to proactively set up your class for success.

Start by Imagining Your Classroom Running Smoothly

Visualize what success looks like: What does it sound like? How are students moving between activities? That vision is your blueprint for success.

Begin with Relationships

Connection is the first layer of prevention. Greet students at the door, learn their names, ask about their day. When students feel seen and valued, they’re more likely to follow guidance and contribute positively to the classroom community.

Routines Are Your Backbone

Disruptions often occur when students are uncertain about what to do next. Set clear, consistent routines for every aspect of the day, from handing in homework to switching stations. Teach them, practice with them, reteach them, and reinforce them daily until they become muscle memory.

Make Expectations Visible

Rather than focusing on rules, focus on expectations. Use visuals, posters, and hand signals to communicate what’s valued in your classroom. Some examples include:

  • We speak respectfully
  • We take turns
  • We help each other succeed

This approach shifts the focus from what students can’t do to what they can do.

Catch It When It’s Good

Praise isn’t just about giving compliments, it's about highlighting what’s working. For example, “I appreciate how quietly Ali lined up” not only reinforces that specific behavior but also teaches other students what success looks like in real time.

  1. What is the FIRST layer of preventing disruptions?
    • ☑ Building strong teacher-student relationships
    • ☐ Enforcing strict rules on Day 1
    • ☐ Ignoring minor misbehaviors
    • ☐ Letting students set all consequences
  2. Why are routines critical for prevention?
    • ☑ They reduce uncertainty and create predictability
    • ☐ They eliminate the need for teacher supervision
    • ☐ They allow students to skip instructions
    • ☑ They help transitions run smoothly
  3. How can teachers make expectations clear?
    • ☑ Use visuals like posters and hand signals
    • ☑ Phrase expectations positively (e.g., "We speak respectfully")
    • ☐ Only mention rules when students break them
    • ☐ Rely on verbal reminders alone
  4. What’s an example of effective praise?
    • ☐ Why can’t you all be like Ali?
    • ☑ I appreciate how quietly Ali lined up, great job!
    • ☐ Finally, someone did it right.
    • ☐ Waiting until the end of class to give feedback
  5. What should teachers visualize when planning preventative strategies?
    • ☑ How the classroom sounds and looks during smooth operations
    • ☐ Only the worst-case scenarios to prepare for disasters
    • ☐ Letting students figure out routines on their own
    • ☐ Skipping practice of routines to save time
  6. How should routines be taught?
    • ☑ Model, practice, reteach, and reinforce daily
    • ☐ Explain once and assume students remember
    • ☐ Only review after misbehavior occurs
    • ☑ Use consistent language and actions
  7. What’s the purpose of "catching it when it’s good"?
    • ☑ Reinforces desired behaviors in real time
    • ☑ Teaches peers what success looks like
    • ☐ Singles out high achievers exclusively
    • ☐ Replaces the need for consequences
